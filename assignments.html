How I achieved the current state

This will only contain what you can see in the current state, not what I did for the prior iterations.

Excuse any incorrect terminology. I am more familiar with Blender and how things are called there.
I also have my application in German, so I don't know the proper English terms.

I started off by creating a sketch for the base plate and extruding it.
Then, I created a sketch representing half the intersection of a pin to hold the base plate in place, rotated it into a mesh, positioned it at a corner of the base plate, and duplicated it with a rectangular displacement, keeping the center spots empty.
After that, I created the pot using a similar method and placed it at the back left-hand corner of the base plate.
Then I went back and created the rest of the shelf with another sketch that I then extruded twice — first to create the back, then to create the frame. Then I rounded the edges.
After that, I created the screen in the shelf by extruding the face into a separate component, insetting the face by 5 millimeters, and pushing the face forward to create the screen.
Then, I created the books by creating a cube, beveling the side edges facing toward the front, and insetting (hollowing) the cube by a few millimeters.
Next, I created another, slightly smaller cube and placed it inside, creating the book look.
Then, using the rectangular duplication tool, I copied the book over.
Next, I created a cube, rounded its corners, placed a cylinder with rounded edges inside, and joined them to carve out a bit in the middle, where I placed another cylinder with rounded edges to create a switch.
Finally, with another sketch, I created the text seen on the side of the books, which I extruded. These words will function as buttons that roll for that check when pressed.

Task 3 - 3D Printing

For this assignment, we had to create and print a model that would be difficult to create with subtractive methods like milling but more easily created with additive methods like 3D printing.

Model Idea

For this assignment, I thought that printing a figurine of my current DnD character would be a good idea.

Not only because I would have needed to print it at some point anyway, as the character is needed for my final project, but also because I believe a small, detailed figurine would be difficult to create subtractively due to the many complex organic shapes that can also intertwine and overlap.

Model Creation


I created my model in Blender.

First, I started out by creating a primitive shape, either a sphere or a cube, which I then sculpted into the head of my character.
I used mirrored sculpting along the X-axis; however, it did not always work perfectly, like for the hollowed-out eyes and eye ridges, and especially the ears.
That wasn't really a problem, however, as no organism would ever truly be perfectly symmetrical.

As I just said, I hollowed out the eye sockets. I filled in the eyes by creating a sphere, moving it into place, and mirroring it.

Similarly, I created a separate object for the tusks in the form of a cylinder, which I also scaled, moved into place, and mirrored. Then, I disabled all mirroring, as only one of the tusks of my character is still intact.
I then sculpted the full tusk into shape.

Next, I created a cube, added and applied a subdivision surface modifier, and used proportional editing to form it into a leg shape, adding loop cuts around it to give it form.

I tried sculpting the hoof nails; however, they did not turn out the way I wanted, so I moved on and later created them with small subdivided round cubes, proportionally edited into shape, then duplicated and placed where I wanted them.

After that, I created a sphere, proportionally editing it into the rough shape of a chubby elephant body (torso).
I then sculpted some more details around the chest and back, generally also defining the shape a little more.

With the head, body, and legs completed, I started giving my character a slightly angled pose, positioning the individual pieces as I saw fit.

Next, I created my character’s hammer by creating a cube and scaling it to be wider. I then beveled the edges.
To create the hilt, I created a cylinder, made it thin and tall, gave it a bunch of loop cuts, and beveled the edges, hiding the lower end within the hammer, eyeballing the center position.

To estimate the right position, I created more cylinders, gave them loop cuts, and formed them a bit into pose using proportional editing.

Having positioned everything, I then added the base plate and positioned it so that the character comfortably sat on top of it.

With all of these elements done, it was time to dress my character, so I created another cylinder, removed its top and bottom, and extruded it inwards.
Switching from edit to sculpt mode, I used the pull brush to pull the dress around the body's contour and to add folds in the fabric.

Next, I created a plane, subdividing it a bunch and adding cloth simulation physics to it.
I then added physics collisions to the body, legs, ground, and dress, simulating how the cloth would fall onto the body.

When I liked the size and look of the robe, I baked the simulation.
However, I felt like one layer of robe didn’t cut it, so I added another subdivided cloth physics plane to the scene, and a collider to the original cloth, simulating how it would fall on top of the other one.

Next, I wanted to add a belt around where the dress and the body meet, so I created a curve, shaped it the way I needed, and converted it to a mesh so I could apply geometry nodes.
Using geometry nodes, I hooked it into a mesh-to-curve, resampled the curve to be more detailed, and then linked it into a curve-to-mesh node.
There, I set the profile curve to a curve circle and hooked up the radius to a noise texture, mapping its values to the radii I wanted, and increased the scale of the noise to make it look more rope-like.

To simulate the robe shaping under the belt's pressure, I added physics collisions to it as well and animated it, making it large at the start, slowly scaling down to normal size.
That way, the cloth would have time to fall down on the body normally and then be tightened around the hips when the belt moved into it.
Therefore, I rebaked the simulations.

To wrap up on the belt, using similar methods, I created another curve, turning it into a knot shape with threads hanging down, following the same curve-to-mesh procedure as before, with the values adjusted.

Next, for the hand that's wrapped around the hammer’s hilt, I created a sphere, sculpting it into shape.
Same for the other hand, but because of its different position, I used a short, long cube as a base instead.

I then adjusted the shape of the arms to fit better, sculpting them into the wide arms of the robe, with gravity pulling down the sleeves and adding folds.

Finally, I created a necklace ring using the same curve-to-mesh method and sculpted another cylinder into the broken-off tusk my character now displays around their neck.

The final models .stl file.

The Print

With my first print complete, I’m quite happy with the overall result. The main features came out nicely, and many fine details are still clearly visible.
The sleeve and facial features printed crystal clear, and even smaller elements like the tusks, trunk, and chain around the neck are well-defined.
The battle hammer, likely thanks to its simple shapes, also retained its details very well.
The legs and hooves are also clearly visible and turned out great.

Some finer details, however — such as the belt laces, the insides of the ears, the robe tips, and the small ripples in the fabric—didn’t survive the print as well.
I realized that my model was simply too small to reproduce those very delicate elements reliably.
Many of these features were also unsupported or suspended in midair, which made them more prone to failure during printing.
If I had used more of the available baseplate area and scaled the model up by around 1.5× to 2×, those details might have printed more successfully.
A slightly larger size would also have reduced the issue of trying to remove thin or fragile parts from the supporting structures.

Despite these small losses in quality during the print, I don't mind all too much for this figurine, as the torn-up robes and ears actually fit the character represented quite nicely - as an undead being.

My main takeaway from this print is to scale up future figurines and other small, detailed models to ensure better durability and sharper results. Overall, I’m pleased with this first attempt and look forward to improving further with these insights in mind.
